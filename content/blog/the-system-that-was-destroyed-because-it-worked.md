In 1971, the British cybernetician Stafford Beer received an unusual invitation. Salvador Allende's newly elected socialist government in Chile wanted help building something that had never existed before: a real-time system for managing a national economy that gave workers and citizens genuine agency over the decisions that shaped their lives.

What Beer built, over the next two years, was called Project Cybersyn. It connected factories across Chile through a telex network, fed production data into a central operations room in Santiago, and gave local managers the information they needed to respond to problems in real time without waiting for instructions from above. The operations room itself - with its distinctive curved chairs and screens displaying economic flows across the country - looked like something from a science fiction film. But the philosophy behind it was straightforward: give people meaningful insight into the systems they are part of, and real democratic agency becomes possible.

It worked. During a strike by lorry owners in 1972, organised with CIA support to destabilise the Allende government, Cybersyn helped coordinate the movement of essential supplies across the country using a fraction of the available vehicles. The system demonstrated that decentralised, worker-led coordination could outperform the centralised command structures it was designed to replace.

On 11 September 1973, General Pinochet's coup overthrew the Allende government. Within days, the operations room was destroyed. The telex network was dismantled. Beer's team fled the country.

Cybersyn was not destroyed because it failed. It was destroyed because it succeeded - because it demonstrated, concretely and practically, that economic power could be distributed to workers and citizens without the sky falling in. That was precisely what the coup's backers, including multinational corporations with significant interests in Chilean industry, could not allow to stand.

## What Beer understood that most democratic theorists miss

Beer was not primarily a political thinker. He was a cybernetician - someone who studied how complex systems maintain viability through feedback, adaptation, and the management of variety. His insight was that democratic governance fails not because people are too stupid or too selfish to participate, but because the systems through which they participate are badly designed.

A parliament that meets periodically, passes laws, and then waits for the next election is not a feedback system. It is a one-shot mechanism that cannot learn, cannot adapt, and cannot respond to the consequences of its own decisions in real time. Beer called this kind of system unviable - not in a political sense but in a technical one. It does not have the structural capacity to match the complexity of the environment it is trying to govern.

What a viable democratic system needs is continuous sensing of what is actually happening, at every level from the local to the national. It needs the capacity to respond to that information quickly, without everything having to pass through a central bottleneck. It needs feedback loops that close - where decisions visibly affect outcomes, and outcomes visibly affect decisions. And it needs to distribute both information and agency, so that the people closest to a problem have the means to respond to it.

This is what Cybersyn was attempting. It is also what direct digital democracy, properly designed, could provide.

## The lesson that was buried with the operations room

The standard history of Cybersyn treats it as an interesting experiment that was cut short before it could be properly evaluated. That framing is too comfortable. Cybersyn was evaluated - by the people who stood to lose most from its success - and they evaluated it as a serious enough threat to destroy along with the government that built it.

That is the lesson worth taking seriously. Systems that genuinely redistribute power do not fail because they are technically flawed. They fail because the people who benefit from the existing distribution of power find ways to stop them. Beer understood this. He spent the rest of his life arguing that the design of democratic systems had to be politically hardened from the start - open source so it could not be captured, decentralised so it could not be switched off, and transparent so that its destruction would be visible to everyone.

Direct digital democracy faces the same environment. The extraction piece on this blog describes how the current political system has been shaped to serve concentrated interests. Those interests do not become less concentrated because a new platform asks citizens what they think. They become threatened - and threatened interests respond.

Building something that can survive that response is not a technical problem. It is the central design challenge. Beer tried to solve it fifty years ago in Chile. The work is not finished.
